QWERTY History

designed to slow you down!

The QWERTY keyboard layout was developed in the late 19th century by Christopher Latham Sholes to address mechanical limitations in early typewriters. Its design aimed to reduce jamming by separating commonly used letter pairs and slowing down typing speed. The QWERTY layout became the standard for typewriters and was subsequently adopted for computer keyboards, where it remains the most commonly used layout today.

In addition to the QWERTY keyboard layout, several alternative keyboard layouts have been developed over the years. These alternatives aim to improve typing efficiency and reduce hand strain. The Dvorak Simplified Keyboard, created by Dr. August Dvorak in the 1930s, is one of the most well-known alternatives. It places the most commonly used letters on the home row, reducing finger movement. Another alternative is the Colemak layout, which maintains a similar key placement to QWERTY but optimizes it for faster and more comfortable typing. Other alternatives include the Workman layout, which focuses on minimizing finger movement, and the Maltron layout, designed to reduce finger stretches. While these alternative layouts have their merits, QWERTY remains dominant due to its widespread adoption and compatibility with existing systems and muscle memory.
